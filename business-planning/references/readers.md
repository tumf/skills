# Reader Optimization for Business Plans

Use this reference when the business plan must persuade a specific main reader.

A business plan is not only analysis.
It is a decision document.
So the same business logic should be presented differently depending on who reads it.

## Core rule

Do not change the underlying logic just to please the audience.
Change:
- emphasis
- ordering
- proof style
- depth
- tone

Keep stable:
- facts
- assumptions
- core strategic choice
- economics

## 1. Founder / internal team

Primary question:
- What should we do next, and what should we not do yet?

What they care about:
- focus
- trade-offs
- execution sequence
- learning speed
- resource realism

Emphasize:
- target customer and wedge
- why this path beats nearby alternatives
- scope discipline
- next 30 / 90 days
- KPI and experiments

Reduce:
- polished storytelling without action relevance

## 2. Executive approver / board

Primary question:
- Should we allocate resources and organizational attention to this?

What they care about:
- strategic fit
- upside vs downside
- capital and people required
- milestones and governance
- portfolio impact

Emphasize:
- why this matters now
- expected business impact
- risk management
- resource ask
- decision gates and review cadence

Reduce:
- overly detailed tactical discussion unless it affects risk or budget

## 3. Investor

Primary question:
- Can this become a large and defensible business worth backing?

What they care about:
- market size
- timing
- wedge
- growth logic
- defensibility
- capital efficiency

Emphasize:
- why now
- why this market can be large enough
- why the team can win
- how the business compounds over time
- what milestones unlock the next round or inflection point

Reduce:
- internal operational detail that does not affect scale or capital needs

## 4. Business partner / enterprise buyer

Primary question:
- Will this solve a real problem with acceptable risk and effort?

What they care about:
- ROI
- time-to-value
- implementation burden
- deployment fit
- support and reliability

Emphasize:
- business pain and measurable benefit
- practical rollout plan
- deployment / compliance fit
- references, proof points, or pilot plan

Reduce:
- abstract market strategy discussion

## 5. Technical evaluator

Primary question:
- Is this feasible and supportable in our environment?

What they care about:
- architecture fit
- integration burden
- performance and reliability
- security and governance
- operational ownership

Emphasize:
- assumptions that depend on technical feasibility
- deployment model
- integration surfaces
- observability and failure modes
- operational boundaries and responsibilities

Reduce:
- generic market language without technical grounding

## Fast adaptation checklist

Before finalizing a plan, answer:
1. Who is the primary reader?
2. What decision must they make?
3. What is their most likely objection?
4. What evidence will reduce that objection?
5. Which section should come earlier for this reader?

## Section-order hints

### Founder / internal team
Suggested order:
- Executive view
- Business plan
- Economics and validation
- Risks and open questions
- Immediate next actions

### Executive approver / board
Suggested order:
- Executive view
- Planning context
- Business plan
- Risks and open questions
- Economics and validation
- Immediate next actions

### Investor
Suggested order:
- Executive view
- Opportunity and market logic
- Business plan
- Economics and growth path
- Why this team can win
- Risks

### Enterprise buyer / partner
Suggested order:
- Executive view
- Customer problem and value proposition
- Offer and implementation path
- ROI / deployment fit
- Risks and mitigation

### Technical evaluator
Suggested order:
- Executive view
- Technical feasibility and constraints
- Offer and integration model
- Operational implications
- Risks and mitigation

## Tone guidance

- Founder / internal team: direct, candid, trade-off aware
- Executive approver / board: concise, risk-aware, resource-conscious
- Investor: ambitious but disciplined
- Enterprise buyer / partner: practical, low-drama, outcome-oriented
- Technical evaluator: precise, constraint-aware, evidence-oriented
